MENTAL HEALTH AND NUTRITION CONNECTION

THE GUT-BRAIN CONNECTION:
- 90% of serotonin (happiness hormone) is made in the gut
- Poor diet leads to poor gut health which affects mood and anxiety
- Probiotic foods improve gut health: yogurt, kefir, kimchi
- Prebiotic foods feed good gut bacteria: garlic, onions, oats, bananas

FOODS THAT BOOST MOOD:
- Dark chocolate (70%+): releases endorphins, contains magnesium
- Fatty fish: omega-3 reduces depression symptoms
- Berries: antioxidants reduce brain inflammation
- Bananas: contain tryptophan which converts to serotonin
- Nuts and seeds: magnesium, zinc, healthy fats support brain
- Leafy greens: folate deficiency linked to depression
- Eggs: contain choline essential for brain health and memory

FOODS THAT WORSEN MOOD AND ANXIETY:
- Sugar: energy spikes and crashes worsen mood and anxiety
- Alcohol: depressant despite initial relaxing effect
- Caffeine (excess): worsens anxiety, disrupts sleep
- Processed foods: linked to higher rates of depression
- Refined carbs: blood sugar instability affects mood

NUTRITION FOR STRESS:
- Magnesium is depleted by stress: eat nuts, dark chocolate, leafy greens
- Vitamin C supports adrenal glands during stress
- B vitamins needed for nervous system: whole grains, eggs, meat
- Avoid turning to junk food during stress (emotional eating)
- Eat regular meals to maintain stable blood sugar and mood

NUTRITION FOR BETTER SLEEP:
- Magnesium promotes relaxation: nuts, seeds, dark chocolate
- Tryptophan promotes sleepiness: turkey, eggs, milk, bananas
- Avoid heavy meals within 3 hours of sleep
- Avoid caffeine after 2pm
- Herbal teas that help sleep: chamomile, valerian, lavender

EATING DISORDERS AWARENESS:
- Anorexia: restricting food intake dangerously
- Bulimia: binge eating followed by purging
- Binge eating disorder: eating large amounts uncontrollably
- These are serious medical conditions requiring professional help
- Do not follow extreme diets or fasting protocols without guidance
- National Alliance for Eating Disorders helpline available for support

BODY IMAGE AND HEALTHY RELATIONSHIP WITH FOOD:
- Food is not the enemy - all foods fit in a balanced diet
- Avoid labeling foods as "good" or "bad" - think "everyday" vs "sometimes"
- Listen to hunger and fullness cues (intuitive eating)
- Do not punish yourself with exercise after eating
- Progress not perfection - consistency over time matters more
